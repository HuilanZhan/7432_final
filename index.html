Missouri expects the potential of wind, while coal is still the top energy contributing to electric grid in the state.


On Nov. 27, Ameren Missouri launched its Renewable Choice Program to help customers receive their energy from renewable wind generation.


“Wind and natural gas are beating coal,” Missouri Energy initiative executive director Josh Campbell said.



Falling wind price


Wind price is falling after reaching a climax in 2009, according to the U.S. Department of Energy (DOE)’s Wind Technologies Market Report. Compared to the price in 2009, wind price in 2017 has decreased 76 percent.


The report projects that wind will become cheaper.


Source: Wind Technologies Market Report 2016


Wind energy resources exhibit the lowest cost on a levelized cost of energy basis among all candidate resource options without tax incentives,” said Ajay Arora, vice president of environmental services and generation resource planning at Ameren.


The Interior region wind price has been lower than national wind price, according to the report.


Source: Wind Technologies Market Report 2016



Rising potential in Missouri


In 2016, cumulative wind capacity in Missouri is 659 megawatts, according to the DOE’s Wind Technologies Market Report. From 2011 to 2015, the capacity in Missouri remains the same for four years, 459 megawatts.


Source: Wind Technologies Market Report 2016


An increasing annual potential generation is projected, with 866,889 gigawatt-hour in 2020 and 982,227 gigawatt-hour in 2050, by a renewable energy company AWS Truepower.


Source: Wind Technologies Market Report 2016


Source: AWS Truepower, NREL Annual Technology Baseline


On September 25, Ameren Missouri announced to investment about one billion dollars in wind and solar generation. Arora said Ameren Missouri has evaluated options for development of wind resources within Missouri and across the broader region.


U.S. Energy Information Administration’s data shows the electric grid mix in Missouri: coal makes up 82.44 percent, following by nuclear, natural gas and wind which makes up 1.29 percent.



Columbia’s considerations


According to previous Missourian reporting, in June Columbia City Council approved a contract with Missouri Joint Municipal Electric Utility Commission, to buy up to 35 megawatts of wind energy from 2021.


Columbia is the first Missouri city to adopt a renewable energy portfolio ordinance, beginning in 2014.


In 2017, wind provides 12 percent of the total amount of energy for Columbia. One percent of that is from a wind farm in Missouri, while the rest of that is from Iowa.


Columbia looks for more possibilities of renewable energy other than wind. “Solar is much more valuable to the utility than wind,” Columbia Utilities Assistant Director Jim Windsor said.


Windsor said that the challenge to traditional energy, such as coal, has been the price of natural gas, which is low because of “fracking” and is projected to remain low.
